About Heart Disease

Evaluation of Symptoms

Patients presenting with certain symptoms may prompt further testing for heart disease. Common symptoms include:

  • Chest Pain or Discomfort: Especially if it radiates to the arm, neck, jaw, or back.
  • Shortness of Breath: Particularly with exertion or while lying down.
  • Palpitations: Irregular or rapid heartbeats.
  • Fatigue: Unusual or severe tiredness.
  • Swelling: Edema in legs, ankles, or feet.

Assessment of Risk Factors

Healthcare providers assess risk factors that increase the likelihood of heart disease. These include:

  • Age: Older age increases risk.
  • Gender: Males are generally at higher risk, though risk increases for women post-menopause.
  • Family History: A family history of heart disease, particularly in close relatives.
  • Medical Conditions: Hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol, and obesity.
  • Lifestyle Factors: Smoking, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, and excessive alcohol consumption.
  • Stress: Chronic stress and mental health conditions.

Physical Examination

A thorough physical examination can reveal signs of heart disease, such as:

  • Heart Murmurs: Indicating potential valvular heart disease.
  • Abnormal Heart Sounds: Such as gallops or rubs.
  • Signs of Heart Failure: Swelling in legs, jugular venous distension, and lung crackles.

Diagnostic Tests

Based on the evaluation of symptoms and risk factors, various diagnostic tests may be ordered:

Non-Invasive Tests

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG): Measures the electrical activity of the heart to detect arrhythmias, ischemia, and myocardial infarction.
  • Echocardiogram: Uses ultrasound to visualize heart structure and function, assessing conditions like heart failure and valvular disease.
  • Stress Test: Assesses how the heart performs under physical stress, identifying ischemia or other abnormalities.
  • Holter Monitor: A portable ECG device worn for 24-48 hours to detect intermittent arrhythmias.
  • Cardiac CT or MRI: Provides detailed images of heart structures, identifying issues such as coronary artery disease or structural abnormalities.

Invasive Tests

  • Cardiac Catheterization and Coronary Angiography: Involves inserting a catheter into coronary arteries to visualize blockages and measure pressures within the heart chambers.
  • Electrophysiology Study: Evaluates the electrical activity and pathways of the heart, useful for diagnosing complex arrhythmias.

Blood Tests

Blood tests can provide critical information regarding heart health:

  • Troponin Levels: Elevated levels indicate myocardial injury or heart attack.
  • Lipid Profile: Measures cholesterol levels, helping assess cardiovascular risk.
  • BNP or NT-proBNP: Elevated levels indicate heart failure.
  • C-reactive Protein (CRP): Elevated levels suggest inflammation, which is a risk factor for heart disease.

Screening Guidelines

Adherence to clinical guidelines helps determine when to screen for heart disease:

  • Routine Screening: Recommended for individuals with multiple risk factors or a family history of heart disease.
  • Age-Specific Recommendations: Guidelines may suggest beginning regular screening at a certain age, especially for those with risk factors.
